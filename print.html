Silver Nanoparticle Antibacterial Activity: Machine Learning Analysis

Cross-Validated Random Forest Model Analysis of 111 Experimental Records with In-Silico Validation

Shreyas Boddani

Grade: 11th Grade, North Forsyth High School, Cumming, GA

Pathway: Computer Science

Email: shreyasboddani@gmail.com

Abstract

This study presents a machine learning-based analysis of silver nanoparticle (AgNP) antibacterial activity using a rigorously curated dataset of 111 experimental records from 18 literature sources. Using 5-fold cross-validation with a Random Forest regressor, our model achieves an average R² score of 0.7154 ± 0.2387 with an RMSE of 331.94 µg/mL. The model provides an in-silico estimate for Dr. Taduri's experimental parameters (18.08 nm, Nothapodytes nimmoniana coating), predicting an MIC of 113.96 µg/mL, consistent with his reported antibacterial activity. Feature importance analysis reveals bacterial susceptibility as the dominant model feature in this dataset (43.3% for Vibrio cholerae), with morphological effects (rod-like shape: 26.1%) and coating properties actively modulating antibacterial efficacy.

1. Introduction & Measurement Alignment

Silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) have gained significant attention due to their broad-spectrum antibacterial activity, making them highly valuable in biomedical applications. Their effectiveness depends fundamentally on physicochemical parameters such as crystallite size, morphology, and surface chemistry (capping agents). The unique properties of nanoscale silver enable critical interactions with bacterial cell membranes, disruption of cellular respiration, and the generation of reactive oxygen species.

Primary Research Question

What are the key determinants of silver nanoparticle antibacterial efficacy, and how can machine learning predict nanoparticle parameters while supporting and extending findings from green-synthesis antibacterial studies?

Measurement Metric Alignment
  • Model Target Metric: Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) in µg/mL
  • Experimental Validation Metric: Zone of Inhibition (ZOI) in mm
  • Mathematical Relationship: MIC and ZOI are inversely correlated. A low predicted MIC is consistent with the experimental observation of a large Zone of Inhibition.

3. Methodology

A Random Forest Regressor framework utilizing 200 decision tree estimators was deployed to capture the non-linear biological interactions between the nanoparticles and bacterial strains. The Random Forest regressor (scikit-learn) used 200 trees, max_depth=None, max_features=sqrt, bootstrap=True, and random_state=42. To ensure robust generalizability and reduce overfitting risk, the model was evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation with random shuffling. Performance metrics extracted include the Coefficient of Determination (R² score), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), and Mean Absolute Error (MAE).

Preprocessing: One-hot encoding was applied to coating, bacteria, shape, and gram_type features. Missing particle_size_nm values were imputed using the dataset median (42.57 nm). The model was trained on raw MIC values; MIC values are strongly right-skewed, and future work could apply log-transformation and calibration.

Cross-validation note: Five-fold cross-validation was stratified only on the samples (no grouping by paper/source), so some studies contribute to multiple folds; this may slightly inflate performance compared to fully external validation.

4. Model Performance

The cross-validated algorithm demonstrated promising predictive capability for a highly variable biological dataset. The 5-fold cross-validation reduces the risk of overfitting, with individual fold R² scores ranging from 0.331 to 0.985, indicating reasonable model stability across different training subsets.

7. Size, Coating, and Bacterial Analysis

Size-Dependent Activity

Quantitative analysis of the dataset shows substantial variation in antibacterial efficacy across particle sizes, which is consistent with literature emphasizing that smaller NPs possess a higher specific surface area, facilitating superior ion release.

  • Small Particles (4-10 nm, n=8): Mean MIC 4.9 µg/mL, median 3.9 µg/mL. MIC range 3.9 - 7.8 µg/mL.
  • Medium Particles (15-25 nm, n=7): Mean MIC 472 µg/mL, median 400 µg/mL. MIC range 25 - 1,560 µg/mL.
  • Large Particles (30-58 nm, n=33): Mean MIC 751 µg/mL, median 320 µg/mL. MIC range 3 - 2,500 µg/mL.

Note: Medium and large size classes show overlapping MIC distributions, indicating that size alone does not determine efficacy.

Bacterial Susceptibility Patterns

The analysis evaluated susceptibility based on cell envelope structure. While literature broadly suggests Gram-negative bacteria are more sensitive due to thinner peptidoglycan layers, this specific dataset highlights that species-level intrinsic resistance (e.g., Vibrio cholerae) can override general Gram-type trends.

8. Scientific Limitations

Dataset & Methodology Constraints
  • Sample Size: 86 explicit MIC points provide moderate statistical power for machine learning.
  • Confounding Variables: Size and coating effects are partially confounded. For instance, the smallest particles all utilized the Camellia tea coating, making it difficult for the algorithm to perfectly isolate the purely dimensional effect versus the chemical effect.
  • Biological Variability: Natural variations in bacterial mutations and slight differences in laboratory MIC vs ZOI methodologies introduce inherent noise (reflected in the ±332 µg/mL RMSE).
  • Algorithm Choice: The necessity of processing small-sample tabular data dictated the use of a Random Forest architecture over deeper neural networks.
  • Cross-Validation Grouping: Cross-validation did not group by paper/source, so some experimental series contribute to multiple folds; this may overestimate generalization slightly.

9. Conclusions and Future Directions

The 5-fold cross-validated model successfully characterizes key relationships governing the antibacterial activity of green-synthesized silver nanoparticles. This dataset suggests an apparent favorable band around 4–22 nm, with 4 nm Camellia-coated particles showing the lowest MICs and 15–25 nm and larger particles generally requiring higher MICs, while indicating that specific plant extracts (like N. nimmoniana and C. sinensis) are associated with high antibacterial efficacy comparable to laboratory AgNP controls in this dataset.

Computational Enhancement to Taduri Research

Beyond retrospective analysis, this model suggests potential directions for optimizing Dr. Taduri's protocol. While the 18.08 nm particle shows effective antibacterial activity, computational analysis indicates that manipulating the extract concentration or reaction temperature to target the 10-15 nm range could be a promising direction for future experimental work.

Final Recommendation

This model provides computational support for Dr. Taduri's N. nimmoniana silver nanoparticle system and suggests that nanoparticle size, coating, and bacterial species jointly influence antibacterial response. The Random Forest model with 5-fold cross-validation on 86 MIC rows offers a data-driven approach to guide nanoparticle optimization.
